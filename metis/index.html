Metis: the people who own themselves

Timeline

1670

The Hudson's Bay Company establishes trade in Rupert's Land. Many traders would marry Indigenous women in order to establish a connection and improve the relationship between the traders and the Native peoples. Their children would grow up in Rupert's land, living next to one another, beginning to develop the Anglophone Metis/Country-born identity.

1750

The French fur traders who traded in the Great Lakes area also married Indigenous women for the same reasons. Communities founded by gens libres (the men who were no longer contracted by a trading company) started to appear, and some of them settled in the Red River region, where the ethnogenesis of the francophone Metis occurred.

1812-1817

The Red River Colony is established and settled by the Earl of Selkirk by an agreement with the HBC. The settlers antagonized the Metis people and the North West Company, cutting off pemmican trade, prohibiting buffalo hunting and attacking rival trade forts.

1816

The Battle of Seven Oaks, which was the climax of an armed conflict between the NWC and the HBC known as the Pemmican War. A band of Metis led by Cuthbert Grant seized a supply of pemmican previously stolen from them from the HBC with the intention of selling it to the NWC. At the site known as the Seven Oaks, they encountered a group of NBC employees and Selkirk settlers. A gunfight ensued, where the Metis won, outnumbering the HBC people almost 3 to 1.

1821

After the merger of HBC and NWC many retired Metis traders settled down in the Red River Colony, increasing Metis presence in the area.

1849

The trial of Pierre-Guillaume Sayer and 3 other Metis traders who had violated the HBC's trading ban. Although he was found guilty, the judge levied no punishment, fearing an armed conflict. This event put the end to the HBC's trading monopoly, effectively allowing free trade in the Assiniboia district.

1851

The Battle of Grand Coteau was fought between the Metis and the Sioux of North Dakota, motivated by the competition between two groups for buffalo. The Metis successfully defended their position, and the victory has become a part of Metis folklore.

1869

Rupert's Land is sold to Canada by the HBC. Metis leaders protested against the notion of their land being transferred without their consent. This triggered an uprising by the Metis to protect their land rights and culture, known as the Red River rebellion. The Metis National Committee and a provisional government are established. Louis Riel becomes the leader of the Metis resistance. The final version of the Metis Bill of Rights is drawn up, intended to be the list of conditions for the entry of Rupert's Land into the Dominion of Canada as a new province.

1870

Allied with the old British settler population, the provisional government sends a delegation to Ottawa. As a result of the negotiations, the Manitoba Act establishes Manitoba as Canada's fifth province.

1870-1880

Metis dispersal from the Red River. The settlers of the newfound province were often hostile to the Metis. As a result, more than two thirds left the area in the following years after the Manitoba Act.

1885

The North-West Rebellion, a five-month insurgency against the government, is fought by Metis and their Aboriginal allies under Louis Riel and Gabriel Dumont. The rebels were defeated by federal troops, and Riel was executed. This event lead to the second Metis dispersal, after which the Metis ceased to be a group of any social or political influence until their re-emergence in the 20th century.
